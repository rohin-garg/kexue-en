Beyond MuP: 4. Adhering to Parameter Stability

By 苏剑林 | April 24, 2026

Through the derivations and calculations in the previous articles, we have found that the three stability indicators proposed in the first post "Beyond MuP: 1. Three Characteristics of a Good Model" can generally be divided into two parts: "Parameter Stability" and "Incremental Stability." In "Beyond MuP: 2. Linear Layers and Steepest Descent" and "Beyond MuP: 3. Special Cases, Special Treatment", we demonstrated the process of combining incremental stability with steepest descent to obtain new update rules (optimizers).

However, for parameter stability, we previously only focused on initialization. The task of this article is to explore how to maintain parameter stability throughout the entire training process, completing the practical application of the theory.

Problem Background

Taking "Beyond MuP: 2. Linear Layers and Steepest Descent" as an example, the three stability indicators are:

Forward stability: $\Vert\boldsymbol{W}\Vert_2 = \Theta(\sqrt{d_{out}/d_{in}})$
Backward stability: $\Vert\boldsymbol{W}\Vert_2 = \Theta(\sqrt{d_{in}/d_{out}})$ (Note: For the weight gradient, this corresponds to different norms)
Dependency stability: $\Vert\boldsymbol{W}\Vert_2 = \Theta(\sqrt{d_{out}/d_{in}})$

Where $\boldsymbol{W}\in\mathbb{R}^{d_{in}\times d_{out}}$ is the parameter of the linear layer. We hope all three indicators are $\Theta(1)$, which means we want the parameters and their increments to satisfy $\Vert\boldsymbol{W}\Vert_2 = \Theta(\sqrt{d_{out}/d_{in}})$ and $\Vert\Delta\boldsymbol{W}\Vert_2 = \Theta(\sqrt{d_{out}/d_{in}})$ respectively. In "Beyond MuP: 3. Special Cases, Special Treatment", we performed calculations for layers like Embedding and LM Head, and the conclusions were similar, though the corresponding norms differed.

The increment condition is treated as a stability indicator. Based on the principle of "seeking speed within stability" from steepest descent, we derive theoretically optimal update rules. For example, the linear layer corresponds to the Muon optimizer:

\begin{equation}\newcommand{argmin}{\mathop{\text{argmin}}}\newcommand{tr}{\mathop{\text{tr}}}\newcommand{msign}{\mathop{\text{msign}}}\argmin_{\Vert\Delta\boldsymbol{W}\Vert_2\leq\eta\sqrt{\frac{d_{out}}{d_{in}}}} \tr(\boldsymbol{G}^{\top}\Delta\boldsymbol{W}) \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \Delta\boldsymbol{W} = -\eta\sqrt{\frac{d_{out}}{d_{in}}}\msign(\boldsymbol{G})\end{equation}

Regarding the parameter stability part, we previously only required that the initialization of parameters satisfies $\Vert\boldsymbol{W}\Vert_2 = \Theta(\sqrt{d_{out}/d_{in}})$. How to ensure that the model maintains the same parameter stability throughout the entire training process remains to be seen.

General Framework

How can we ensure that $\boldsymbol{W}$ maintains $\Vert\boldsymbol{W}\Vert_2 = \Theta(\sqrt{d_{out}/d_{in}})$? More generally, given a parameter $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ (which could be a vector, matrix, or even a higher-order tensor), a norm $\Vert\cdot\Vert$ (usually induced by forward stability or dependency stability), and a scale $\tau$, the question is: how can we make $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ maintain $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}\Vert=\Theta(\tau)$ during training?

Preliminary Thoughts

A naive idea is to directly force $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}\Vert=\tau$ (where $\tau$ could also be replaced by its constant multiple, but this doesn't affect the following discussion). The simplest implementation is to rescale the norm to $\tau$ via normalization after each optimization step (e.g., Hyperball and Nemotron-Flash). Another approach is to directly reparameterize the original model using normalization, i.e., changing $\boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{x};\boldsymbol{\omega})$ to $\boldsymbol{f}(\boldsymbol{x};\tau\boldsymbol{\omega}/\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}\Vert)$, which theoretically achieves a similar effect.

A more advanced approach is to combine the idea of steepest descent to adjust the update rule, as discussed in the articles "Steepest Descent on Manifolds: 1. SGD + Hypersphere", "Steepest Descent on Manifolds: 4. Muon + Spectral Sphere", and the paper "Controlled LLM Training on Spectral Sphere". This is methodologically more elegant but more complex in practice, usually requiring the solution of a non-linear equation to obtain the exact update amount.

However, should we really strictly control the norm of a parameter to a specific value? Intuitively, the norm of a parameter should be decided by the training process itself; we should at most set a prior range for it. Although some work shows that if set correctly, fixing the parameter norm to a preset value does not hurt performance, it still breaks the original training dynamics and might require more effort to understand and adapt to.

Therefore, the viewpoint proposed in this article is that we only need to ensure $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}\Vert = \mathcal{O}(\tau)$. Specifically, we aim to ensure $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}\Vert \leq \tau$ at each step. As for the exact value—whether it reaches $\Theta(\tau)$—that should be left to the training algorithm to decide without further intervention.

Post Clip

The next question is: how to implement $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}\Vert \leq \tau$? More specifically, assume the original update rule for $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is:

\begin{equation}\boldsymbol{\omega}_t = \boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1} - \eta \boldsymbol{\phi}_t\label{eq:base-update}\end{equation}

How should this be modified to ensure $\boldsymbol{\omega}_t$ always satisfies $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_t\Vert\leq\tau$? There are many methods; for example, the normalization mentioned in the previous section is one. Given this, we want to choose the scheme that has the least impact on the optimization process. That is, given parameter $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and norm $\Vert\cdot\Vert$, we want to apply the smallest modification to make its norm no greater than $\tau$, formally defined as:

\begin{equation}\color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{\omega}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert\leq\tau}} = \argmin_{\Vert\tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\Vert\leq\tau} \Vert \boldsymbol{\omega} - \tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\Vert_{RMS}\label{eq:nclip}\end{equation}

Readers familiar with convex optimization will easily recognize this as a projection operation onto a hypersphere of a certain norm with radius no larger than $\tau$. The key point here is that we achieve the goal of the norm not exceeding $\tau$ while minimizing the impact on the original parameter $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ by minimizing the difference metric $\Vert \boldsymbol{\omega} - \tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\Vert_{RMS}$, which induces a specific projection or clipping operation.

As for how to calculate $\color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{\omega}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert\leq\tau}}$, it requires specific analysis for different norms, which we will expand on later. With this operation, one scheme we can consider is "Post Clip": after every update, truncate the parameter norm using this operation, i.e., changing Eq. $\eqref{eq:base-update}$ to:

\begin{equation}\boldsymbol{\omega}_t = \color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1} - \eta \boldsymbol{\phi}_t\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert\leq\tau}}\end{equation}

This scheme is simple and intuitive, but it might feel "non-smooth." This is understandable: suppose the initialization radius is less than $\tau$; as training starts, the radius slowly increases. Once it reaches $\tau$, the clipping "suddenly" triggers. Although this process is continuous, it is not smooth, similar to the $\max(x,0)$ function.

Pre Decay

If one minds this non-smoothness, one can consider imitating weight decay by distributing the penalty across every update step. Starting again from the update rule $\eqref{eq:base-update}$, assume $\boldsymbol{\phi}_t$ satisfies $\Vert \boldsymbol{\phi}_t \Vert \leq \tau$. According to the triangle inequality, $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_t\Vert = \Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1} - \eta \boldsymbol{\phi}_t\Vert \leq \Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1}\Vert + \eta \tau$. In the extreme case, the norm increases by $\eta\tau$ at each step, which could lead to "going out of control" over time.

To prevent this, we can give $\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1}$ a little pre-processing before subtracting $\eta \boldsymbol{\phi}_t$ to reduce its norm, just enough to counteract the growth brought by the update. Based on experience with weight decay, we can consider:

\begin{equation}\boldsymbol{\omega}_t = \color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert\leq (1-\eta)\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1}\Vert}} - \eta \boldsymbol{\phi}_t\label{eq:pre-decay}\end{equation}

In other words, we first find a way to reduce the norm of $\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1}$ to $1-\eta$ times its original value, and then perform the update. This way:

\begin{equation}\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_t\Vert \leq (1-\eta)\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1}\Vert + \eta \tau \leq \max(\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1}\Vert,\tau)\end{equation}

Propagating this yields $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_t\Vert \leq \max(\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1}\Vert,\tau) \leq \cdots \leq \max(\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_0\Vert,\tau)$. Thus, as long as the initialization satisfies $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_0\Vert\leq\tau$, the entire update chain automatically satisfies $\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_t\Vert\leq \tau$. This conclusion is independent of the specific norm; it only relies on the triangle inequality. The operation that minimizes changes while reducing the norm is exactly the clipping operator defined in Eq. $\eqref{eq:nclip}$.

We call this scheme "Pre Decay." The difference from "Post Clip" is that the latter's threshold is static ($\tau$), so clipping might not always trigger. In "Pre Decay," the threshold is dynamic ($(1-\eta)\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1}\Vert$), and the "clipping" (norm reduction) always triggers. This process is smoother, hence we call it decay rather than clipping; it is a general generalization of weight decay.

Basic Results

So far, we have established a general framework for constraining parameter norms, with "Post Clip" and "Pre Decay" schemes. The core operation is the clipping operator $\color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{\omega}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert\leq\tau}}$ defined in Eq. $\eqref{eq:nclip}$. We currently only have a formal definition; in practice, it needs to be calculated for specific norms. Below are some basic results.

Simple Example

In this section, we first calculate a simple example where the chosen norm is $\Vert\cdot\Vert_{RMS}$. For vectors, this is equivalent to the L2 norm; for matrices, it is equivalent to the Frobenius (F) norm. It is not difficult to obtain:

\begin{equation}\color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{\omega}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert_{RMS}\leq\tau}} = \argmin_{\Vert\tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\Vert_{RMS}\leq\tau} \Vert \boldsymbol{\omega} - \tilde{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\Vert_{RMS} = \min\left(1,\,\frac{\tau}{\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}\Vert_{RMS}}\right)\boldsymbol{\omega}\end{equation}

The proof is left to the reader (if you really can't figure it out, ask Kimi). In particular, substituting $\tau = (1 - \eta) \Vert\omega\Vert_{RMS}$ gives:

\begin{equation}\color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{\omega}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert_{RMS}\leq (1 - \eta) \Vert\omega\Vert_{RMS}}} = \min\left(1,\,\frac{(1 - \eta) \Vert\omega\Vert_{RMS}}{\Vert\boldsymbol{\omega}\Vert_{RMS}}\right)\boldsymbol{\omega} = (1-\eta)\boldsymbol{\omega}\end{equation}

Substituting this into Eq. $\eqref{eq:pre-decay}$ yields:

\begin{equation}\boldsymbol{\omega}_t = (1-\eta)\boldsymbol{\omega}_{t-1} - \eta \boldsymbol{\phi}_t\end{equation}

As can be seen, this is conventional weight decay. In other words, Pre Decay under the RMS norm is exactly the weight decay we commonly use. It is the modification-minimal Pre Decay scheme for maintaining the RMS norm (equivalently, the L2 norm for vectors or the F norm for matrices) constraint.

Singular Clipping

Now we enter the "main event" of this article—matrix parameters and Muon. Here we use the notation $\boldsymbol{W}$ and write Muon's original update rule as:

\begin{equation}\boldsymbol{W}_t = \boldsymbol{W}_{t-1} - \eta\lambda\boldsymbol{\Phi}_t,\quad \boldsymbol{\Phi}_t=\frac{1}{\lambda}\sqrt{\frac{d_{out}}{d_{in}}}\msign(\boldsymbol{G}_t)\end{equation}

Let $\tau = \frac{1}{\lambda}\sqrt{\frac{d_{out}}{d_{in}}}$, then $\Vert\boldsymbol{\Phi}_t\Vert_2=\tau$. The two schemes to keep $\boldsymbol{W}_t$ satisfying $\Vert\boldsymbol{W}_t\Vert_2\leq\tau$ are:

\begin{align} \text{Post Clip:}\quad\boldsymbol{W}_t =&\, \color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1} - \eta\lambda\boldsymbol{\Phi}_t\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert_2\leq\tau}} \\[5pt] \text{Pre Decay:}\quad\boldsymbol{W}_t =&\, \color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert_2\leq(1-\eta\lambda)\Vert\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1}\Vert_2}} - \eta\lambda\boldsymbol{\Phi}_t \ \end{align}

The next task is to calculate $\color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{W}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert_2\leq\tau}}$. Given the equivalence of RMS and F norms, this is equal to:

\begin{equation}\color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{W}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert_2\leq\tau}} = \argmin_{\Vert\tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}\Vert_2\leq\tau} \Vert\boldsymbol{W} - \tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}\Vert_F\label{eq:mclip-loss}\end{equation}

The optimal solution to this problem may be familiar to some readers. It is the "Singular Value Clipping (SVC)" mentioned in "High-order MuP: Simpler but Smarter Spectral Condition Scaling", and referred to as $\newcommand{mclip}{\mathop{\text{mclip}}}\mclip$ in "Calculating Singular Value Clipping mclip via msign (Part 1)" and "Calculating Singular Value Clipping mclip via msign (Part 2)":

\begin{equation}\color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{W}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert_2\leq\tau}} = \mclip(\boldsymbol{W};\tau) = \boldsymbol{U}\min(\boldsymbol{\Sigma},\tau)\boldsymbol{V}^{\top}\label{eq:2-to-mclip}\end{equation}

Where $\boldsymbol{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\boldsymbol{V}^{\top}$ is the SVD of $\boldsymbol{W}$, and $\min(\boldsymbol{\Sigma},\tau)$ clips the singular values to not exceed $\tau$. We will demonstrate the proof in the next section. With this notation, the two schemes can be written as:

\begin{align} \text{Post Clip:}\quad\boldsymbol{W}_t =&\, \mclip(\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1} - \eta\lambda\boldsymbol{\Phi}_t;\tau) \\[5pt] \text{Pre Decay:}\quad\boldsymbol{W}_t =&\, \mclip(\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1};(1-\eta\lambda)\Vert\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1}\Vert_2) - \eta\lambda\boldsymbol{\Phi}_t \ \end{align}

Derivation Process

In this section, we prove Eq. $\eqref{eq:2-to-mclip}$. Let the SVD of $\boldsymbol{W}$ be $\boldsymbol{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\boldsymbol{V}^{\top}$, where $\boldsymbol{U}\in\mathbb{R}^{d_{in}\times d_{in}}$, $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\in\mathbb{R}^{d_{in}\times d_{out}}$, and $\boldsymbol{V}\in\mathbb{R}^{d_{out}\times d_{out}}$. Then:

\begin{equation}\Vert\boldsymbol{W} - \tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}\Vert_F = \Vert\boldsymbol{U}\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\boldsymbol{V}^{\top} - \tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}\Vert_F = \Vert\boldsymbol{U}(\boldsymbol{\Sigma} - \boldsymbol{U}^{\top}\tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}\boldsymbol{V})\boldsymbol{V}^{\top}\Vert_F = \Vert\boldsymbol{\Sigma} - \boldsymbol{U}^{\top}\tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}\boldsymbol{V}\Vert_F\end{equation}

The last equality holds because orthogonal matrices do not change the Frobenius norm. Similarly, orthogonal matrices do not change the spectral norm. Let $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}=\boldsymbol{U}^{\top}\tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}\boldsymbol{V}$. The objective in Eq. $\eqref{eq:mclip-loss}$ can be simplified to:

\begin{equation}\argmin_{\Vert\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\Vert_2\leq\tau} \Vert\boldsymbol{\Sigma} - \tilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\Vert_F\end{equation}

Note that $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is a diagonal matrix with diagonal elements $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \cdots \geq 0$, but $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}$ is currently undetermined and must be treated as a general matrix for the proof. Using component notation:

\begin{equation}\Vert\boldsymbol{\Sigma} - \tilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\Vert_F^2 = \sum_i \sigma_i^2 + \sum_{i,j} \tilde{\Sigma}_{i,j}^2 - 2\sum_i \sigma_i \tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i} \geq \sum_i \sigma_i^2 + \sum_i (\tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i}^2 - 2 \sigma_i \tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i})\end{equation}

Looking at each term, $\tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i}^2 - 2 \sigma_i \tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i}$ is just a quadratic function of $\tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i}$, minimized at $\tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i}=\sigma_i$. However, we have the constraint $\Vert\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}\Vert_2\leq\tau$. Since the spectral norm is greater than or equal to the absolute value of any element in the matrix, we at least have $\tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i}\leq\tau$. Under this constraint, the minimum of $\tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i}^2 - 2 \sigma_i \tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i}$ is attained at $\tilde{\Sigma}_{i,i}^* = \min(\sigma_i,\tau)$.

By ensuring all inequalities become equalities, we get $\tilde{\Sigma}_{i,j}^*=0$ for $i\neq j$. Thus $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}^*$ is also a diagonal matrix, which can be concisely written as $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}^*=\min(\boldsymbol{\Sigma},\tau)$. This corresponds to $\tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}^*=\boldsymbol{U}\min(\boldsymbol{\Sigma},\tau)\boldsymbol{V}^{\top}$. The proof of Eq. $\eqref{eq:2-to-mclip}$ is complete.

Clipping Leading Terms

So, how can we efficiently compute $\mclip$? Performing an SVD at each training step is obviously too expensive. In Part 1 and Part 2 of the $\mclip$ via $\msign$ series, we systematically discussed this, suggesting implementations using $\msign$, which require 2–3 $\msign$ operations and are costly. For example, a derived identity was:

\begin{equation}\mclip(\boldsymbol{W};\tau) =\frac{1}{2}\Bigl\{\boldsymbol{W}+\tau\msign(\boldsymbol{W})-(\tau\boldsymbol{I}-\boldsymbol{W}\msign(\boldsymbol{W})^{\top})\msign(\tau\msign(\boldsymbol{W})-\boldsymbol{W})\Bigr\}\end{equation}

This requires two $\msign$ operations. Since parameter calculations are often in FP32, executing $\msign$ twice is still quite expensive and not particularly viable.

Here we mainly consider the term-by-term clipping approach discussed in "Muon Implementation via Streaming Power Iteration: 5. Extensions". Specifically, $\mclip$ changes all singular values greater than $\tau$ to $\tau$. A necessary operation is to change the leading singular value to $\tau$ (if it is greater than $\tau$). After clipping the leading singular value, if there are still singular values greater than $\tau$, the largest among them becomes the new leading singular value. Thus, repeatedly "clipping the leading singular value to $\tau$" can implement $\mclip$.

Since the leading singular value and vectors can be efficiently found via power iteration (denoted as $\mathop{\text{SVD1}}$), leading singular value clipping can be considered efficient. Furthermore, assuming the training is smooth enough, we can perform just one leading singular value clipping per step to approximately achieve the same effect. Based on this strategy, the two spectral norm constraint schemes can be written as:

\begin{align} \text{Post Clip:}\quad\boldsymbol{W}_t =&\, \tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}_t - \max(\sigma_1 - \tau, 0) \boldsymbol{u}_1 \boldsymbol{v}_1^{\\top},\quad\sigma_1, \boldsymbol{u}_1, \boldsymbol{v}_1 = \mathop{\text{SVD1}}(\tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}_t),\quad\tilde{\boldsymbol{W}}_t = \boldsymbol{W}_{t-1} - \eta \boldsymbol{\phi}_t \\[5pt] \text{Pre Decay:}\quad\boldsymbol{W}_t =&\, \boldsymbol{W}_{t-1} - \lambda\eta\sigma_1 \boldsymbol{u}_1 \boldsymbol{v}_1^{\\top} - \eta \boldsymbol{\phi}_t,\quad\sigma_1, \boldsymbol{u}_1, \boldsymbol{v}_1 = \mathop{\text{SVD1}}(\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1}) \end{align}

The "Pre Decay" version is exactly the Spectral Weight Decay introduced in "Reflections from Spectral Norm Gradients to a New Style of Weight Decay". Over a year later, we have arrived at the same result from a different path. As for the "Post Clip" version, @_arohan_ once mentioned it on X, calling it "Wion." In practice, since only one singular value is clipped per step, some "ambitious" matrices might have spectral norms that deviate significantly from the set threshold. This is normal and will slowly decrease during the LR Decay phase.

Other Details

For more precise clipping, we can use power iteration to find the Top-$k$ singular values and vectors simultaneously, clipping at most $k$ singular values per step. The cost is that the L2 Normalize in power iteration must be replaced with QR decomposition. QR decomposition also has acceleration methods—for related principles, refer to the streaming power iteration series, such as "Muon Implementation via Streaming Power Iteration: 1. Introduction".

Besides the spectral norm of linear layer matrices, in "Beyond MuP: 3. Special Cases, Special Treatment" we encountered different norms for other layers. For example, Embedding and LM Head correspond to the max RMS of rows and columns, respectively. The $\gamma$ parameter in RMS Norm corresponds to the maximum absolute value, also known as the infinity norm of a vector.

Fortunately, the clipping operators $\color{skyblue}{\lfloor}\boldsymbol{\omega}\color{skyblue}{\rfloor}_{\color{skyblue}{\Vert\cdot\Vert\leq\tau}}$ for these norms are quite easy to calculate. For the Embedding layer, the norm is the maximum row RMS, so the clipping operator truncates each row's RMS to not exceed $\tau$. The same applies to the LM Head, but for columns. As for the $\gamma$ parameter, it's even simpler: direct element-wise clipping $\mathop{\text{clip}}(\boldsymbol{\gamma};-\tau,\tau) = \max(\min(\boldsymbol{\gamma},\tau),-\tau)$.

These conclusions are intuitive and the proofs are simple, so we won't expand on them; consider them as exercises for the reader.

Necessary Guarantees

Some readers might wonder: is it necessary to be this complex? Can't we just use ordinary weight decay like in "Training Deep Learning Models with Norm-Constrained LMOs"? For example:

\begin{equation}\boldsymbol{W}_t = (1-\eta\lambda)\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1} - \eta\sqrt{\frac{d_{out}}{d_{in}}}\msign(\boldsymbol{G}_t)\label{eq:muon-wd}\end{equation}

This can also theoretically limit the spectral norm within $\tau = \frac{1}{\lambda}\sqrt{\frac{d_{out}}{d_{in}}}$. Why not use this simple form?

The answer is: to avoid excessive intervention. From the definition in Eq. $\eqref{eq:nclip}$, our defined clipping operator is the operation that applies the smallest change to the original parameter while achieving the same constraint effect. For the spectral norm, directly multiplying by $1-\eta\lambda$ may make the spectral norm of $\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1}$ no greater than $(1-\eta\lambda)\Vert\boldsymbol{W}_{t-1}\Vert_2$, but since it differs from the minimal-change $\mclip$, it necessarily involves some degree of "excessive intervention."

Excessive intervention has two consequences: either one chooses a small $\lambda$ to ensure performance, which makes $\tau$ too large to guarantee the spectral norm stays within the desired range; or one chooses a large $\lambda$ to control the spectral norm, but this significantly degrades performance. For example, if $d_{in}=d_{out}$ and we want the spectral norm to not exceed 5, then $\lambda=0.2$ according to the formula. For Muon in Eq. $\eqref{eq:muon-wd}$, a weight decay coefficient of 0.2 is extreme (usually it's around 0.01).

Note that we emphasize "guarantees" multiple times. This is crucial. Suppose we use weight decay with a coefficient of 0.01; theoretically, the spectral norm could reach 100, but experiments on small models might show it doesn't even reach 5. This is common. However, small model safety does not imply large model safety. As we've said before, large models are powerful enough to amplify any subtle bug. If the theoretical upper bound is 100, small models might never reach it, but large models truly might.

Therefore, it is very necessary to maintain a reasonable theoretical bound for the key norms of parameters. This is the manifestation of "stability" in the "seeking speed within stability" principle. The clipping operator defined in Eq. $\eqref{eq:nclip}$ is the "lightest" operation to ensure boundedness; in other words, it is likely the operation that minimizes performance loss while ensuring the same bound.

Article Summary

Based on the idea of minimal modification, this article proposes a general framework for maintaining parameter stability during training, including two schemes: Post Clip and Pre Decay. Under the spectral norm, these evolve into singular value clipping and spectral weight decay. These operations aim to ensure the boundedness of key parameter norms while minimizing interference with training dynamics.
