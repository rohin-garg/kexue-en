Continue Observing the International Space Station

By 苏剑林 | July 29, 2009

If you see a bright star moving in the night sky, you might guess it's an airplane. However, please add another hypothesis: it might just be the "International Space Station (ISS)"!

A post about observing the International Space Station was previously published at https://kexue.fm/archives/14/. However, that post focused on article translation; this one is an introduction to the actual observation of the space station.

To observe it, you first need to know when the International Space Station will pass over your head and how long it will stay; otherwise, it's all talk!

Below is the location map of the International Space Station (refresh to see the latest, click to enlarge):

To accurately find the pass times for your specific location, you first need to know your local latitude and longitude. Many websites currently provide this query, for example: http://astro.lady.qq.com/a/20050616/000062.htm

Through lookup, I found that my location (Xinxing, Yunfu, Guangdong) is at 22.68°N, 112.2°E.

Now, modify the following URL:

http://www.heavens-above.com/PassSummary.aspx?lat=Latitude&lng=Longitude&tz=ChST

Generally, you only need to modify the latitude and longitude, while &tz=ChST represents China Standard Time!

After modifying and opening the link, we can see:

Included details are appearance time, disappearance time, direction, brightness (around -2, which is quite bright), and so on.

Alright, you can grab your telescope and camera and get ready for action! However, the appearance time is short, so you must be quick when observing!

Appendix: The International Space Station and Space Shuttle "Joint" Solar Transit:

According to the US "Wired" magazine website, a French photographer recently captured a spectacular image of the US space shuttle "Endeavour" joined with the International Space Station during a solar transit after docking.

You might not be able to take such a photo with your digital camera, because the French photographer and amateur astronomer Thierry Legault, famous for taking astronomical photos, captured this spectacular scene through a special solar filter; he is a standout among photographers.
