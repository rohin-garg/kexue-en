Exquisite Craftsmanship: Calculating the Volume of an n-dimensional Ball

By 苏剑林 | December 23, 2014

This morning, a classmate asked me about the relationship between the Gamma function and the volume of a ball in $n$-dimensional space. I remember wanting to study this before, but I never followed through. Since she brought it up, let's complete this unfinished plan.

Standard Approach

Simply put, the volume of an $n$-dimensional ball is the following $n$-fold integral:

\[V_n(r)=\int_{x_1^2+x_2^2+\dots+x_n^2\leq r^2}dx_1 dx_2\dots dx_n\]

Using a more geometric approach, we can use a set of parallel planes (specifically, $(n-1)$-dimensional parallel hyperplanes) to cut the $n$-dimensional ball into a series of approximate small hypercylinders. From this, we can derive a recursive formula:

\[V_n (r)=\int_{-r}^r V_{n-1} \left(\sqrt{r^2-t^2}\right)dt\]

Let $t=r\sin\theta_1$, then we have:

\[V_n (r)=r\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} V_{n-1} \left(r\cos\theta_1\right)\cos\theta_1 d\theta_1\]

Iterating once gives:

\[V_n (r)=r^2\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} V_{n-2} \left(r\cos\theta_1\cos\theta_2\right)\cos\theta_1\cos^2\theta_2 d\theta_1 d\theta_2\]

Iterating $n-1$ times:

\[\begin{aligned}V_n (r)=&r^{n-1}\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\dots\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} V_1\left(r\cos\theta_1\cos\theta_2\dots \cos\theta_{n-1}\right)\times\\ &\cos\theta_1\cos^2\theta_2\dots\cos^{n-1}\theta_{n-1} d\theta_1 d\theta_2\dots d\theta_{n-1}\end{aligned}\]

Where $V_1 (r)=2r$, which is a line segment of length twice the radius. Thus:

\[V_n (r)=2r^{n}\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\dots\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \cos^2\theta_1\cos^3\theta_2\dots\cos^{n}\theta_{n-1} d\theta_1 d\theta_2\dots d\theta_{n-1}\]

By completing this integral, we eventually obtain the formula for the volume of an $n$-dimensional ball. This integral can indeed be solved (it is simply a product of $n-1$ one-dimensional integrals). However, these steps are quite tedious. Furthermore, to connect it with the Gamma function, much more work is required. Overall, this is not an easy-to-remember or particularly elegant standard method.

Brilliant Approach

There is a brilliant technique utilizing Gaussian integrals that allows us to directly connect the volume of the ball to the Gamma function. The entire process is a piece of exquisite craftsmanship and feels like a unique, "one-of-a-kind" solution. It is said that this trick is well-known to physics students. I first saw it on Baidu Wenku, and the original source is "Thermodynamics and Statistical Mechanics" by Greiner (Germany), Example 5.2: Statistical Calculation of the Entropy of an Ideal Gas.

This ingenious line of reasoning begins with calculating a Gaussian integral in two different ways:

\begin{equation}G(n)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\dots\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \exp\left(-x_1^2-x_2^2-\dots-x_n^2\right)dx_1 dx_2 \dots dx_n\tag{1}\end{equation}

On one hand, we treat $(1)$ as $n$ iterated integrals. Since we have already calculated (see here):

\[\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\exp(-t^2)dt=\sqrt{\pi}\]

And since $(1)$ is merely the product of $n$ such integrals, we have:

\begin{equation}G(n)=\pi^{n/2}\tag{2}\end{equation}

On the other hand, we treat $(1)$ as an $n$-fold integral. Since the integrand depends only on the radial distance $r=\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2+\dots+x_n^2}$, it is natural to think of spherical coordinates. In $n$-dimensional space, these are called "hyperspherical coordinates." We don't need to write out the full coordinate transformation; we only need to note that an integral over the ball can be transformed into an integral over "spherical shells" followed by an integral over the radius:

\begin{equation}G(n)=\int_{0}^{+\infty}dr\int_{S_n(r)}\exp\left(-r^2\right)dS_n\tag{3}\end{equation}

Where $S_n(r)$ represents the surface of an $n$-dimensional ball of radius $r$ (and its surface area; for simplicity, we don't distinguish between the two here). Since the integrand depends only on $r$, integrating over the surface is equivalent to multiplying the function by the surface area of the ball. Thus, Equation $(3)$ becomes:

\begin{equation}G(n)=\int_{0}^{+\infty}dr\exp\left(-r^2\right)S_n(r)\tag{4}\end{equation}

Although we do not yet know the formulas for the volume and surface area of an $n$-dimensional ball, we can be certain that the volume $V_n(r)$ must be proportional to $r^n$, i.e.,

\[V_n (r)=V_n(1)r^n\]

The surface area $S_n(r)$ is the first derivative of the volume with respect to the radius (considering the shell decomposition):

\[S_n (r)=n V_n(1)r^{n-1}\]

Substituting this into $(4)$, we get:

\begin{align} G(n)=&n V_n(1)\int_{0}^{+\infty}r^{n-1}\exp\left(-r^2\right)dr \nonumber \\ =&\frac{1}{2}n V_n(1)\int_{0}^{+\infty}(r^2)^{n/2-1}\exp\left(-r^2\right)d(r^2) \nonumber \\ =&\frac{1}{2}n V_n(1)\int_{0}^{+\infty}z^{n/2-1}\exp\left(-z\right)dz\quad\left(z=r^2\right) \nonumber \\ =&\frac{1}{2}n V_n(1)\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}\right) \tag{5} \end{align}

Combining this with $(2)$, we have:

\[\pi^{n/2}=G(n)=\frac{1}{2}n V_n(1)\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)\]

Thus:

\[V_n(1)=\frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\frac{1}{2}n\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)}=\frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)}\]

Finally, the formula for the volume of an $n$-dimensional ball is:

\[V_n(r)=\frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)}r^n\]

And there we have it! By differentiating with respect to $r$, we obtain the surface area formula for an $n$-dimensional ball:

\[S_n(r)=\frac{2\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)}r^{n-1}\]

Comparing the results of the two methods, we also find that:

\[\frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma\left(\frac{n}{2}+1\right)}=2\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\dots\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}}\int_{-\frac{\pi}{2}}^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \cos^2\theta_1\cos^3\theta_2\dots\cos^{n}\theta_{n-1} d\theta_1 d\theta_2\dots d\theta_{n-1}\]

Simple Commentary

This technique is remarkably beautiful and concise. Gaussian integrals and spherical coordinate transformations are tools very familiar to physics students. With a simple change of perspective, the result is calculated—it truly feels like a brilliant insight that only a physics student would come up with!

Even more fascinating is that this line of reasoning is so ingenious that one might want to apply it to more problems. However, a little further research reveals a startling conclusion: it can't really do anything else! In other words, the entire process seems custom-tailored solely for calculating the volume of an $n$-dimensional ball. Truly "one-of-a-kind"! Magnificent.
